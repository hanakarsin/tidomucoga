Voters Should Shun Mr Moneybags Politicians, All Known Thieves of Public Resources

 Posted on May 9, 2024  |  4 minutes  |  762 words  |  Almeda Bohannan

A new study on the cost of running for political office in Kenya has laid bare some disturbing facts about the country’s political organizing and implications for development.

According to the research by leading governance experts Karuti Kanyinga and Tom Mboya, the more a person spends, the greater their chances of winning elections.

To win a Senate seat, for instance, aspirants spend an overage of KSh 35.5 million.

Secondly, many aspirants turn to politics as an avenue for personal enrichment and aggrandizement; contrary to the electorate’s expectation of service delivery by the elected leaders.

With 40% of those gunning for political office coming from the corporate world, elections in Kenya are now a matter of profits.

The study findings speak to what Kenyans have been treated to by the political class. We have witnessed some presidential aspirants dolling out millions of shillings to interest groups, and churches with little political philosophy or cogent development blueprints.

Commercialization of politics in Kenya carries underlying undesirable outcomes that the electorate should be alive to.

As the cost of competitive elections goes up, we are likely to see more money being splashed to entice the voters.

The sources of funds are usually undeclared, a situation made worse by lack of capping on the amounts politicians can spend during campaigns.

The flashing generosity is often bankrolled by proceeds of corruption, even as the electorate is caught up in short term gratification that only gives way to cyclic trepidations.

This brand of politics has dehumanized Kenyans who no longer expect organized, sustainable and fruitful service delivery from the politicians.

Many politicians and state officers have in the recent past been investigated for alleged pilfering of public resources through shady contracts, undelivered goods and overprized tenders.

Only this week, we have witnessed flamboyant legislator was charged in court for fraudulently receiving billions of shillings.

While some of the accused persons have since been pushed out of office on the weight of accruing evidence, the same faces are now busy lining up for elective posts as if nothing happened.

Asked if there is a lesson that Kenyans could learn from Zimbabwean politics, one of the candidates interviewed for the IEBC commissioner job simply answered that she wished less money was involved in our politics.

With a focus on money, more gifted and service-oriented candidates are swept under the rugs with little opportunity to ascend to leadership positions.

Interestingly, according to the study, while women candidates often outspend their male counterparts, fewer women are elected.

As the 2022 polls draw closer, people with questionable integrity are busy endearing themselves to the electorate by using ill-gotten wealth.

Tokenism has become modus operandi with Kenyan youth taunting aspirants for handouts. As a result, politicians are now buying their way into the presidency, parliament and county assemblies.

Once in office, elected leaders use their newfound position to lobby for personal gain as a way of replenishing lost resources to campaigns.

Since most of the elected have no business serving the country, they continue with the culture of tokenism which only breeds more appetite to raid public coffers.

A compromised voter cannot make the right choice at the ballot. Similarly, a compromised legislator cannot offer effective oversight to the executive.

Even worse, a corrupt executive does not just steal money; softer aspects of corruption breed compromised public service with a plethora of social ills. Electoral integrity is therefore a key ingredient of democratic governance and spring well of development.

To ring-fence the democratic gains so far consolidated, Kenyans must begin by shunning politicians whose only tool of the campaign is money. Known thieves of public resources shouldn’t be allowed to thrive in our elective politics.

This is not just a role for the electorate. The investigative agencies should ensure that people who have profited from public resources are held to account, as a way of creating a new discourse towards clean, productive and issue-based politics.
