Analysis: By-elections have proved William Ruto cant ignore regional kingpins, influential govern

One of the takeaways from the just concluded seven by-elections is that the place of regional kingpins in the country’s politics cannot be ignored.

The mini polls emphasized the phrase “all politics is local”, which encapsulates the principle that a politician’s success is directly tied to his ability to understand and influence the issues of his constituents.

According to pundits, the fact that voters in Kabuchai and Matungu constituencies, as well as those in Makueni’s Kitise/Kithuki ward widely heeded the advice of their own ethnic chiefs when electing local leaders, must shake individuals with presidential ambitions to reality ahead of 2022.

Of particular interest is Deputy President William Ruto, who has been raiding various regions across the country without the blessings of local supremos.

He has since 2018 made forays into Mount Kenya, prompting repeated cautions from President Uhuru Kenyatta that he avoids using the backdoor if he wants to secure political support from the region.

Though Ruto has chosen to ignore the warnings, the performance of the United Democratic Alliance (UDA), the party he associates with after all but abandoning the Jubilee Party, should serve as a lesson that he cannot take regional kingpins for granted.

In the mini polls, Musalia Mudavadi (Amani National Congress), Moses Wetang’ula (Ford Kenya) and Kalonzo Musyoka (Wiper Democratic Movement) successfully fought off attempts by Ruto to encroach into their respective backyards.

The immense time and resources the DP had invested in the campaigns failed to sway the outcomes in his favour.

In Matungu, Amani's victory proved Mudavadi is still king in Kakamega. The party’s candidate, Peter Nabulindo, was declared the winner after garnering 14,257 votes to beat off a strong challenge from ODM’s David Were, who got 10,565 votes. UDA's Alex Lanya came a distant third with 5,513 votes.

The win not only ensured that Mudavadi warded off attempts by Ruto to make inroads into his backyard but also proved that the former deputy premier can hold his own against Raila Odinga’s ODM, which has dominated the region’s politics for long.

In Kabuchai, the locals heeded Wetang’ula’s call during the campaigns that they vote for the Ford Kenya candidate so as not to embarrass him.

“Do not embarrass me among my peers by voting in a person who is not from my party in my home turf,” he had appealed on the campaign trail.

Ford Kenya’s Joseph Majimbo Kalasinga emerged the winner with 19,274 votes, trouncing UDA’s Evans Kaikai who was a distant second with 6,455 votes.

Analysts contend the win was proof that the senator is no pushover in his Bungoma backyard, one of the regions that Ruto covets.

“It is a plus for Wetang’ula because he can now go out and bargain with other partners at the national front with Bungoma as his stronghold,” political commentator Isaac Wanjekeche remarked in the wake of Wetang’ula’s impressive victory.

Wiper’s victory in Kitise/Kithuki ward proved that Kalonzo is still the Ukambani supremo despite onslaughts from Governors Kivutha Kibwana, Alfred Mutua and Charity Ngilu, on one hand, and UDA, whose leader is former Machakos Senator Johnstone Muthama, on the other.

In the by-election, Wiper’s Sebastian Muli garnered 3,892 votes with Muungano Party's Joseph Kioko coming second with 878 votes.

Kibwana is the Muungano chairman and has declared presidential ambitions though he lately appears to have shifted support to former Cabinet minister Mukisha Kituyi.

While Mudavadi, Wetang’ula and Kalonzo Musyoka join Uhuru (Mt Kenya), Ruto (Rift Valley) and Raila Odinga (Nyanza) on the list of regional kingpins, an interesting emerging trend shows that second-term governors are increasingly gaining influence over regions without obvious kingpins.

In Kiamokama ward in Kisii, a region that presently does not have a first among equals since former Cabinet minister, the late Simeon Nyachae, retired from active politics, ODM retained the seat thanks to a strong election campaign principally spearheaded by Governor James Ongwae.

The seat was among those that UDA was eyeing with keen interest as the Gusii region is one of the swing vote areas Ruto has invested in, making numerous trips there in readiness for next year’s presidential contest.

But thanks to Ongwae’s effort, Kisii perceivably remains an ODM zone going into next year’s elections.

The Governor assumed the role of acting MCA to see through key projects the late Kenneth Mainya was implementing in his ward, spearheaded campaigns and easily delivered the seat to Malack Matara. UDA’s candidate Moses Nyakeramba came a distant sixth.

“The people of Kiamokama, thank you for heeding the @TheODMparty call. Your voice has been heard. It is Malack Matara Mainya, it is ODM! To the entire @TheODMparty team and all our supporters I salute you. Chama iko imara! Congratulations Hon. Matara!” Ongwae tweeted after the announcement of results.

Notably, the victory of independent candidate Feisal Bader in the recent Msambweni constituency by-election was also mainly thanks to a governor, Kwale’s Salim Mvurya. Ruto only won by association.

With the coast region lacking a kingpin, Mvurya faced off with Mombasa governor Hassan Joho in a popularity contest and won hands down. Joho enjoys similar popularity in Mombasa, and so does Amason Kingi in the neighbouring Kilifi.

Other influential second term governors include Turkana’s Josphat Nanok, Wycliffe Oparanya (Kakamega) and Mwangi Wa Iria (Murang’a).

The emergence of strong governors, coupled with the flexing of political muscles by regional kingpins, will no doubt generate excitement as alignments and realignments take shape ahead of the 2022 elections.
